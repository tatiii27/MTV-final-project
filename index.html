Africa

1960–1970:

In the 1960–1970 period across Sub-Saharan Africa, only about 11% of secondary-school-age girls were enrolled in secondary school, compared with roughly 16% of boys. University was almost out of reach for most: tertiary enrollment hovered around 1% for women and 2% for men. Women lived to about 44 years on average, just under 3 years longer than men, while fertility was extremely high at around 6.7 births per woman and more than 150 births per 1,000 teenage girls.


2010–2020:

By 2010–2020, secondary enrollment had risen to about 39% for girls and 46% for boys, and tertiary enrollment to roughly 7.5% for women and 10% for men—still a sizable gender and global gap. Women’s life expectancy climbed to about 61 years, about 3–4 years longer than men’s, but fertility remained high at around 4.9 births per woman and over 100 births per 1,000 teenage girls. Only a bit more than half of births were attended by skilled health staff, highlighting persistent inequalities in health and socio-economic conditions.

Asia

1960–1970:

In South Asia around 1960–1970, secondary education was heavily skewed toward boys: only about 13% of girls were in secondary school compared with more than 32% of boys. At the university level, women’s gross enrollment averaged under 2% versus about 7% for men. Women actually lived slightly shorter lives than men (about 44.5 vs 45.8 years on average), while fertility was close to 6 births per woman and teen fertility exceeded 115 births per 1,000 girls, reflecting early marriage and childbearing.


2010–2020:

By 2010–2020, the picture had changed dramatically: secondary enrollment reached about 66% for both girls and boys, and the secondary gender gap nearly disappeared. Tertiary enrollment rose to roughly 21% for women and 22% for men, narrowing the higher-education gap. Women’s life expectancy increased to about 70 years, now about 2½ years longer than men’s, while fertility fell to around 2.5 births per woman and teen fertility dropped to about 32 births per 1,000 girls. Still, only about 62% of births were attended by skilled health staff, showing ongoing inequities in maternal care.

Middle East & North Africa

1960–1970:

In the Middle East & North Africa during 1960–1970, secondary education was limited for everyone and especially for girls: only about 18% of girls were enrolled in secondary school compared with roughly 35% of boys. University participation stood at around 4% for women versus 8% for men. Women lived to about 50.5 years on average, about 1½ years longer than men, while fertility averaged nearly 6.9 births per woman and teen fertility was very high at around 135 births per 1,000 girls.


2010–2020:

By 2010–2020, access to education had expanded dramatically: secondary enrollment reached about 77% for girls and 81% for boys, and at the tertiary level women slightly outnumbered men (around 38% vs 37% gross enrollment). Women’s life expectancy rose to about 75 years, roughly 3½ years longer than men’s, while fertility fell to about 2.9 births per woman and teen fertility to around 40 births per 1,000 girls. Nearly 89% of births were attended by skilled health staff, signaling big improvements in maternal care even as gender gaps persist in other domains.

Europe

1960–1970:

In Europe & Central Asia in the 1960–1970 period, secondary schooling was already widespread: about 80% of girls and 83% of boys were enrolled in secondary education. At the tertiary level, women’s gross enrollment averaged around 24% compared with about 25% for men—relatively small gaps by global standards. Women lived to about 71.5 years on average, roughly 6½ years longer than men, with fertility around 2.7 births per woman and teen fertility around 41 births per 1,000 girls.


2010–2020:

By 2010–2020, Europe & Central Asia had essentially universal secondary schooling, with gross enrollment above 100% for both girls and boys (reflecting older students in the system). The big shift is in higher education: women’s tertiary enrollment climbed to about 72%, compared with roughly 61% for men, putting women firmly ahead in university access. Women’s life expectancy rose to about 81 years versus 74 years for men, keeping a gender gap of roughly 6½ years, while fertility declined to about 1.7 births per woman and teen fertility fell below 20 births per 1,000 girls. Nearly all births—around 99%—were attended by skilled health staff.

Latin America & the Caribbean

1960–1970:

In Latin America & the Caribbean in 1960–1970, only about 27% of secondary-school-age girls and 29% of boys were in secondary school. At the tertiary level, gross enrollment averaged about 5% for women and 9% for men. Women lived to around 60.5 years, roughly 4 years longer than men, while fertility was high at roughly 5.6 births per woman and teen fertility topped 100 births per 1,000 girls.


2010–2020:

By 2010–2020, the region had transformed its education landscape: secondary enrollment reached about 97% for girls versus 91% for boys, and women pulled decisively ahead in higher education with tertiary enrollment near 54% compared to about 42% for men. Women’s life expectancy rose to about 78 years, around 6½ years longer than men’s, while fertility fell to roughly 2.1 births per woman. Teen fertility, though improved, remained high at about 66 births per 1,000 girls. Around 93% of births were attended by skilled health staff, reflecting substantial but incomplete progress in health and socio-economic equity.

Northern America

1960–1970:

In North America during 1960–1970, detailed data on secondary gross enrollment are limited, but tertiary education already showed a clear male advantage: university enrollment averaged about 39% for women compared with roughly 56% for men. Women lived to about 74 years on average, roughly 7 years longer than men. Fertility was near 3 births per woman, and teen fertility was high at around 69 births per 1,000 girls, reflecting the post-war baby boom and limited reproductive autonomy.


2010–2020:

By 2010–2020, secondary schooling had become nearly universal, with gross enrollment close to 99% for both girls and boys. The gender pattern in higher education flipped: women’s tertiary enrollment rose to about 102% (gross) versus roughly 74% for men, meaning women are now far more likely to be in college or university. Women’s life expectancy increased to about 81.5 years, still about 5 years longer than men’s, while fertility fell to about 1.8 births per woman and teen fertility dropped to around 23 births per 1,000 girls. Nearly all births (about 99%) were attended by skilled health staff, underscoring high overall access to maternal care alongside persistent gendered health risks for men.

Oceania

1960–1970:

In Oceania (averaging Australia and New Zealand) in 1960–1970, secondary education was already widespread: roughly 76% of girls and 77% of boys were enrolled in secondary school. At the tertiary level, however, men were much more likely to attend: women’s gross enrollment was about 12% compared with about 20% for men. Women lived to around 74 years, about 6 years longer than men, while fertility averaged roughly 3.4 births per woman and teen fertility was about 55 births per 1,000 girls.


2010–2020:

By 2010–2020, secondary schooling reached very high levels for both genders, with gross enrollment exceeding 125% for girls and boys (again reflecting older students in the system). The biggest shift is in higher education: women’s tertiary enrollment soared to about 111%, far above men’s roughly 78%, making women the clear majority in university. Women’s life expectancy climbed to about 84 years, about 4 years longer than men, while fertility fell below 2 births per woman and teen fertility dropped to around 17 births per 1,000 girls. Almost all births—around 98%—were attended by skilled health staff, reflecting strong health systems even as gendered patterns in education and longevity continue to evolve.
